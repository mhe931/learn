Data Warehousing Methodologies

The foundation of a traditional data warehouse lies in its design methodology. The two most prominent approaches are from Bill Inmon and Ralph Kimball.

Inmon vs. Kimball

Bill Inmon: Corporate Information Factory (CIF)

  • Approach: Top-down, centralized.
  • Core Idea: A normalized (3NF) enterprise data warehouse (EDW) acts as the single source of truth. Data marts are then built from the EDW for specific departmental analysis.
  • Analogy: A central hub-and-spoke model. The EDW is the hub.
  • Pros: High data integrity, low redundancy, single version of the truth.
  • Cons: Higher initial complexity and cost, slower to implement.

Ralph Kimball: Dimensional Modeling

  • Approach: Bottom-up, decentralized.
  • Core Idea: Data marts are built first, focused on specific business processes. These are designed using dimensional models (star schemas). The EDW is the union of these data marts.
  • Analogy: A collection of interconnected business-focused marts.
  • Pros: Faster time-to-value, more intuitive for business users, flexible.
  • Cons: Can lead to data redundancy and "silos" if not managed carefully (the "conformed dimensions" are key).

Advanced Modeling Techniques

Data Vault 2.0

Data Vault is a hybrid methodology that takes the best of both Inmon (3NF) and Kimball (star schema). It's designed for agility, flexibility, and auditability.

  • Core Components:
    • Hubs: Represent core business keys (e.g., CustomerID, ProductID).
    • Links: Represent the relationships between Hubs.
    • Satellites: Store descriptive attributes about Hubs or Links, tracking changes over time (similar to SCD Type 2).
  • Pros: Easily scalable, supports parallel loading, full audit trail of data.
  • Cons: Can result in a large number of tables, requires more joins for end-user queries (often an information mart is built on top).

Anchor Modeling & 6NF

Anchor Modeling is an extremely normalized approach, adhering to the 6th Normal Form (6NF). Every attribute for an entity is stored in its own table.

  • Core Idea: Maximizes flexibility to add new attributes without changing existing structures. It is ideal for highly volatile data models.
  • Structure: Consists of Anchors (like Hubs), Attributes, Ties (like Links), and Knots (for pre-defined value sets).
  • Pros: Unmatched flexibility, non-destructive model changes, full temporal history.
  • Cons: Massive number of joins required for queries, making it impractical for direct analysis without an abstraction layer.

Review & Exam Prep

  • Inmon vs. Kimball is a classic debate. Know the core difference: Inmon is top-down (central EDW first), Kimball is bottom-up (data marts first).
  • Data Vault is the agile, auditable middle-ground. Remember its three core components: Hubs, Links, and Satellites.
  • Anchor Modeling (6NF) is about extreme normalization for maximum flexibility, but at the cost of query complexity.
  • Be ready to explain why you would choose one model over another based on a business scenario (e.g., a large bank needs Inmon's integrity, a startup needs Kimball's speed).
